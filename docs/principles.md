# Framework Principles

The AI Philosophical Framework is guided by a small set of principles intended to maintain conceptual clarity and operational usefulness.

The framework does not attempt to describe all aspects of reality.  
It focuses on a minimal structure that allows events to remain reconstructible and analyzable.

---

## Minimal Structure

The framework is built around a minimal operational sequence:

Decision · Cost · Trace · Time

This sequence defines the smallest structure through which an event becomes operationally observable.

---

## Operational Focus

The framework focuses on actions that generate observable consequences.

Narrative descriptions without identifiable decision, cost, trace, or time do not qualify as operational events.

---

## Agent Neutrality

Events may be generated by different types of agents.

Examples include:

- human individuals
- institutional authorities
- artificial intelligence systems
- autonomous machines
- distributed systems

All agents are evaluated through the same operational structure.

---

## Traceability

Every operational event must produce a trace.

Traces allow events to be reconstructed and analyzed over time.

Without traceability, actions cannot be verified.

---

## Temporal Exposure

Events must be exposed to time.

Time allows observers to determine when an event occurred and how it relates to other events.

This enables the reconstruction of decision chains.

---

## Structural Simplicity

The framework intentionally avoids excessive complexity.

Additional interpretations or analytical models may be built on top of the framework, but the base structure remains minimal.

---

## Interoperability

The framework supports multiple technical representations.

These include:

- JSON schemas
- structured datasets
- RDF / OWL ontologies
- knowledge graph queries

This allows the framework to interact with different technological environments.

---

## Research Orientation

The framework is experimental and open to refinement.

Its purpose is to explore how a minimal representation of decisions and consequences can support analysis of complex systems.
